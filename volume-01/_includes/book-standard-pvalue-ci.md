### Book-standard wording: *p*-values and confidence intervals

Use this wording consistently across the handbook:

**P-value:** A *p*-value describes how **incompatible** the observed data are with a specified statistical model **under the null hypothesis**. It is **not** the probability that the null hypothesis is true, **not** the probability that the finding occurred “by chance,” and **not** a measure of clinical importance.

**Respiratory analogy:** A low *p*-value is like an unusual spirometry trace **under the assumption** that equipment, manoeuvre quality, patient classification, and reference equations are all appropriate. It prompts attention; it does not by itself establish diagnosis or treatment benefit.

**95% confidence interval:** A realised 95% CI is **not** a 95% probability that the fixed true effect lies inside this particular interval. It is a range of effect sizes **compatible with the data and model** under repeated sampling.

Ask three questions when you read a CI:

1. **Compatibility:** Which effect sizes remain plausible?
2. **Precision:** Is the interval narrow enough for the decision at hand?
3. **Clinical span:** Does the interval include materially beneficial **and** harmful effects?

**Effect sizes:** Prefer **natural units** in respiratory work (mL FEV₁, exacerbations per person-year, absolute risk difference) over standardised effects (Cohen’s *d*) when the scale is clinically meaningful.
