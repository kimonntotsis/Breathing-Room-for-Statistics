# Part V: Structure Discovery (many markers) {.unnumbered}

Unsupervised methods are where overclaiming is most common in respiratory research.

This part teaches a defensible discovery workflow:

1. **Reduce dimensions** (Ch 10) to visualize and stabilize noisy marker panels
2. **Cluster defensibly** (Ch 11): stability, batch checks, endotype claim ladder
3. **Integrate end-to-end** (Ch 12): write publication-style narratives and say what was *not* proven

Ch 10 and Ch 11 include “alternatives menus” so you can choose methods based on marker type (continuous, binary, mixed), dimensionality (p>>n), and goal (exploration vs supervised reduction).

**Read this if:** you have a marker panel or unsupervised clustering result and need stability, batch checks, and honest “hypothesis-generating” language.

**Skip this if:** your paper is a conventional RCT with FEV1 primary endpoint (→ [Parts II–IV](parts/part-02-describe-compare.md)); return here when biomarker subgroups appear.

## CASTOR vignette: the marker panel meeting

A postdoc presents PCA and k-means on CASTOR’s 30-marker panel. Clusters separate beautifully: but colour by `processing_batch` shows the same separation. **Part V** is how you turn a pretty slide into a defensible discovery story: reduce dimensions honestly (Ch 10), cluster with stability checks (Ch 11), and write what Case C in Ch 12 does *not* prove.
